Curriculum Vitae

15-17:   EuroFusion fellowship at CCFE with Prof S L Dudarev
11-15:   PhD at Imperial College London under Prof A P Sutton FRS

Many properties of real materials are linked to the dynamics of (point, line and surface) defects in their crystalline order. Defect motion often requires thermal agitation, occuring on wildly varying timescales of nanoseconds to months. Sometimes the slowest processes are irrelevant, sometimes they control macroscopic phenomena.

I develop theoretical and computational methods to model materials, typically starting at the atomistic level. The goal is to find the important information which can connect to experiment, either directly or via phenomenological models. Traditionally this selection is based on domain expertise and scientific intuition; a current focus is combining this with statistical measures of uncertainty, allowing simulations to be automatically selected, distributed and analyzed on massively parallel computers much more rapidly. Most applications to date have focused on the ageing of metals post irradiation.

      Openings

  • Postdoc in uncertainty-driven exploration of defective materials
    • A central problem when modelling real materials is that defective structures can arrange themselves in a fantastic variety of highly non-intuitive configurations, which are hard or impossible to guess a priori. This is a serious issue, as the thermodynamics and kinetics of crystal defects controls numerous phenomena in materials science and catalysis. Whilst existing knowledge and expertise should always be harnessed, traditional simulation approaches rely on extensive end-user analysis to determine the quality of a given simulation, which is expensive, unscalable and prone to bias.

    We are offering a 20-24 month postdoc appointment to execute, analyze and extrapolate high-throughput, massively-parallel simulations of point defect processes in materials, primarily using the proposer's TAMMBER code (see software and this paper). The first goal of the project is generate a large database of point defect diffusion processes relevant for irradiation damage and surface science, across a range of material models.

    The second goal will be to analyze this database to look for global trends, using Bayesian regression techniques. Depending on the expertise of the candidate, this information will then be used at higher or lower scales, in conjuction with collaborators in France, the USA and the UK. Visits to collaborators are planned. The lower scale goal is to use the database analysis to improve the training of machine-learning based interatomic potentials, which will involve some ab initio calculation. The higher scale goal is to build data-driven materials models with quantified uncertainty, which will involve some spectral analysis of master equations.

    The candiate should have:
    A PhD in the sciences with a strong computational/theoretical component
    Some experience with atomistic simulations, ideally of materials
    Experience with parallel computation / job submission
    Experience with Python+numpy+scipy, bash, git etc

    The ideal candiate would have:
    Some knowledge of rate theory / rare events
    Experience coding in C++ (or F90 etc)
    Familiarity with master equations
    Some experience with ab initio computation
